What is the UBC Academic Calendar and why is it important?
  • The UBC Academic calendar is where you can find your degree requirements, information regarding awards, distinctions, learn more about co-op, dates for the winter session, summer session and exam periods, and any academic policies or regulations at UBC.
  • Please keep in mind the 2019/20 UBC Academic Calendar has all of the official policies, regulations, dates and information that UBC students are responsible for this year.
  • We recommend you bookmark this link and/or bookmark specific sections of the academic calendar that is relevant to you such as your degree requirements. This is because every year, the previous academic calendar gets archived and this can make it more difficult to search for it so we recommend you bookmark this page.
What is my CWL and why is it important?
  • Your CWL (Campus-Wide Log-in) provides you with access to many important UBC services such as the Student Service Centre (SSC), Canvas (course management system) and virtually any other UBC-hosted programs to give you access to information you would not have if you were not a UBC student (e.g. access to many academic journals that are paid by UBC to give you free access to).
  • You also need it to log-in to the UBCsecure wi-fi and having access to UBCsecure also means you can access some additional services.
  • Please ensure your CWL has a secure password (8-12 characters with a number and a special character) and you will be asked to periodically update this password as well. Do not sure your password with anyone to keep your personal information safe!
What is the SSC and why is it important?
  • The SSC (Student Service Centre) is where you are able to find all of your personal information as a student at UBC, including some very important processes such as:
    • Course registration & Degree navigator
    • Paying tuition and fees
    • Final grades
    • Final exam schedules
    • Declaring your major
    • Housing applications
    • Awards and scholarships
    • Apply for graduation
    • Order transcripts
    • Changing majors/degrees
  • Ensuring your have a secure CWL password also means that other people cannot access your personal student information.
Where can I get my UBC Card and what is it?
  • You can get it at the Access Desk at the back of the UBC Bookstore or submit it online (and pick it up at the Access Desk or "Express Pick-Up" booth during August and September usually near the bookstore).
  • This acts as your library card along with other benefits and perks.
  • It includes your student number and acts as an important piece of identification that you need for your final exams.
How do I log into the campus wi-fi and why should I choose UBCsecure?
  • UBCsecure provides you with access to some services you wouldn’t have on another wi-fi network such as some room booking services.
  • You use your CWL to log-in to UBCsecure so ensure you do this for all of your devices that you will be using on campus.

What can I expect my classes to be like?
  • During the first half of your degree, you will likely be in courses that are lectures with hundreds of students. Each week, you will usually have 3 hours of lecture.

  • You can read more about lectures, tutorials, labs and tutorials here. Where can I buy textbooks?
  • Consider waiting until the first class to see if you need the textbook and/or you can buy an older/used version! This can end up saving a lot of money in the end, especially for science textbooks that include an answer key - these two books can be $100 each
  • To save money on textbooks, it is a good idea to check whether the books you need are available at any of these places:
    • UBC Textbooks 4 Sale (online)
    • Buy & Sell - UBC Textbooks (online)
    • UBC Secondhand (online)
    • Books2Go UBC (online)
    • Discount Textbooks at the University Village (in-person)
  • When you’re purchasing textbooks used from another person, ask them:
    • The condition of the book (e.g. writing, notes, highlighting, missing pages, etc)
    • Edition of the book (double check you can use this one for your course)
    • Their asking price (they may ask you to offer but if possible, ask them the price and it may be lower than you expected)
    • Set up a specific date, time and location in a public area
    • Exact cash (if you are selling or buying) to make the exchange easier
    • Exchange an e-mail or phone number as you deem appropriate in case they need to contact you on the day of the exchange
  • Tips:
    • Compare prices of other used books by searching the course and/or textbook name in any of the facebook group search bars to see what the approximate value is so you’re not being overcharged for the book
    • Look early and message swiftly because the used textbooks can be bought very quickly
    • You can try to offer a slightly lower price (e.g. asking for $50 and you offer $40 or $45) but beware if the book is popular or there aren’t many other sellers, that you may have to take it for the offer price
    • Beware of interac-e transfers (mainly when you sell textbooks), cash is the easiest. Bring along change with you in case the buyer doesn’t have exact cash (but try to emphasize this in the initial conversation)
    • There may be a chance that after agreeing with you to the exchange, that they back out due to another person paying more. In this case, consider the other facebook groups, Discount Textbooks and UBC Bookstore.

What is the U-Pass program and where can I buy my CompassCard?
  • The U-Pass program is a subsidized 3-zone, seabus and skytrain transit pass for all students at UBC. Students pay $41/month that they are in school as part of the school fees which are mandatory (ie. if you’re not taking summer classes, you don’t get the discount) compared to $174/month.
  • You can buy a Compass Card ($6 deposit) at the UBC Bookstore or Shoppers Drug Mart or other locations near you.
  • You will need to bring your student ID with you when you use the transit system to prove you are in the U-Pass program.
  • Each month you have to request your monthly pass and you can request it online by going to this link: https://upassbc.translink.ca/
    • Reloading your card ensures that you are using your transit pass and that it is always available to you. If you forget to request your monthly pass, it can take up to 24 hours for it to register once you request it using the link.
    • It may be a bit weird to have to reload it every month but that is how monthly passes are processed onto your Compass Card whether you are a part of the U-Pass program or not.
    • You can reload your card on the 21st of every month, so make sure to create a reminder for yourself!
  • Contacts:
    • For technical issues with your Compass Card, visit www.compasscard.ca/Help or contact Compass Customer Service Centre at (604) 398-2042.
    • For U-Pass inquiries, contact the AMS Transit & External Research Commissioner at upass@ams.ubc.ca.
What buses run to campus?
  • *Remember to have your Compass Card with you and loaded with the monthly pass!
  • For buses coming to campus, look for ones labelled “UBC” (e.g. 4, 14, 41, 33, 9, 99 B-line, 84, etc) because this means their last stop is at UBC campus. You will get dropped off at the UBC bus exchange near the Aquatic Centre.
  • One of the key buses is the 99 B-Line, it comes frequently (every 3-4 minutes on weekdays and during peak times) and has a lot of space). Since this bus does not have many stops, it can get you to UBC quicker than some other buses that stop a lot more (such as the 4 or 14). Use google maps to see what buses work for you and where you are coming from!
  • For buses leaving campus, you can use google maps and it is pretty accurate (even with construction and the bus stops moving around) to see which bus stops you need to go to and/or ones that are nearest to where your destination is.
    • Going to downtown you may want to take the 4 or 14 (potentially the 44)
    • Going to VCC-Clark station you’ll likely take the 84
    • Going to Commercial-Broadway station (along with Broadway-Cityhall Skytrain station and Granville Street) you’ll likely want to take the 99 B-line)
    • Double check your route on google maps and see what options you have!
  • Some bus etiquette if you haven’t taken the bus a lot:
    • Priority seating for pregnant, different-abled, strollers, kids and elderly near the front of the bus (denoted by yellow triangles on the relevant walls of the bus)
    • Remove your backpack or bag to give more space to everyone else
    • Move to the back if the bus is full (which it likely will be at peak times - 8-10am and 3-6pm) make sure you move all the way to the back so everyone can fit in
    • Say thanks to the bus driver as you leave
  • The 68 and 70 are shuttles that go around UBC campus every 30 minutes. These shuttles are useful if you need to go from campus to Thunderbird stadium, Doug Mitchell Thunderbird Sports Centre, Wesbrook village and the UBC Farm. It is also useful if you need to go from one side of campus to the other side to access the bus exchange.

What is Degree Navigator and is it actually accurate?
  • Degree Navigator (sign in through your SSC -> Registration -> Degree Navigator - Vancouver) gives you an estimate of how far along you are to meeting your requirements for your degree.
  • You can use this as a loose guide and always make sure to check with an Academic Advisor to make sure you’re on track. This is especially important if you change majors or faculties. You will receive access to this tool in your 2nd year.
How many credits per year should I take to finish my degree? How long will it take usually to finish my degree?
  • This depends on your major which you can find out on the  2019/20 Academic Calendar.
  • Reducing their course load and/or taking courses in the summer can help you be more successful in the courses you have. However, just be aware of the rules for maintaining your housing, study visa, and/or student loans and scholarships, if  its applicable to you.
  • You may think about graduating as quickly as possible but consider enriching your experience on-campus, in the community or internationally. You never know where you will discover your passion and you can learn valuable and transferable skills along the way! It is quite common to graduate in 5 years now because a lot of students are studying abroad, doing Science co-op, guided independent learning opportunities, and other opportunities to gain experience. These experiences will also make you more confident and more competitive in the job market once you graduate so think about how you would like to enrich your experience and talk to an Academic Advisor at LFS Student Services if you need some advice.
I'm registered in a course that I don't like anymore, but the Add/Drop deadline has passed, what should I do?
  • While you can drop a course without penalty before the Add/Drop deadline, once it has passed dropping a course would leave a W on your transcript.
  • Even if you don’t like a course you can still learn and grow from the experience, you may need it as a prerequisite for another course or your degree program. If you do not like the course and/or struggling in the course, talk to your instructor, an academic advisor at LFS Student Services (drop-in or call) or teaching assistant (TA) to get help.

What skills would help me in managing my coursework?
  • Video Playlist - Crash Course Study Skills: This playlist holds a variety of videos to support you in your learning. Try out some of the strategies and see what works for you. Be mindful that it will take some time to adjust to these new skills and refine them over time.
    • Taking Notes: Why you should take notes via pen and paper, focusing on big ideas, bullet lists, terms and definitions, examples* (very important for math, physics or chemistry where you need to apply formulas!), note taking methods (outline, cornell, mind-mapping).
    • Reading Assignments: How you read, speed reading, pseudo-skimming technique, active reading, SQ3R (survey, question, read, recite, review)
    • Memory: Sensory memory, long-term memory, retrieval strength, spacing effect, leitner system, active recall.
    • Planning & Organization: Organization: organization system (Task manager, calendar, note-taking system, physical storage), scheme on how to keep things organized (folder structure - tree system), quick capture or daily note and planning strategies.
    • Focus & Concentration: Multitasking myth, break your tasks down, take breaks regularly and remembering to relax.
    • Procrastination: Short-term and long-term rewards, breaking down tasks, ask for help, rewarding yourself, willpower and the pomodoro technique.
    • Studying for Exams: Exact dates, times and locations of exams, context-based studying (e.g. under a time limit, quiet place, no notes, etc), active studying, how to ask for help, cheat card exercise, take breaks.
    • Test Anxiety: Strategies to manage test anxiety, the three big fears (fear of past failures, fear of the unknown, fear of the stakes), negativity bias, writing out your worries and more.
    • Papers & Essays: Prewriting phase, research process, first draft, content edit technical edit and ask other people to read your paper.
    • Exercise & Learning: Optimize neurotransmitter, don’t study when exercising, exercise right before you study, just get started, focus on building the habit.

Study Skills

How should I approach quizzes (small stake assessments)?
  • Try not to miss one of them and leaving enough time to put in quality effort. Usually small stake assignments happen more often (usually weekly) so plan your time accordingly to be able to balance these because you will likely have multiple weekly small assessments that you need to do.
  • Using a to-do list app or another medium means you can write reminders each week (automated in apps whereas you have to do it manually if you’re using a planner or notebook). Consider your schedule and when you will have time to complete the work.
How should I approach assignments (large stake assessments)?
  • Starting early. By starting early you have more time to plan out what you need to get done and when (break it down into smaller tasks), you also have more time to double check your work, make your revisions and edits.
  • If it is a project, report or paper it is vital to start early and seek help when you need it so you don't get overwhelmed.
What are some effective study strategies?
  • Just reading your notes or the textbook is not gonna work! BUT academic success is possible! We will clear the air here that learning in university is more independent and you will be challenged in these exams on a deeper level than you were in highschool. Using effective study strategies, asking for help and knowing how to study each of your courses is what will make you successful in university.
  • The Learning Center at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill has described some key study strategies that can lead you to academic success which you can learn more about here:
    • Use the syllabus
    • Use your prior knowledge
    • Think aloud
    • Ask yourself questions
    • Use writing
    • Organize your thoughts
    • Take notes from memory
    • Review your exams
    • Take breaks
    • Test yourself
    • Figure out how you learn
  • There are a few videos you may be interested in watching:
    • 3 min video - The 9 BEST Scientific Study Tips by ASAPScience

    • 4 min video - How to Learn Faster by ASAPScience

How should I study for midterms? How do I study for final exams?
  • You want to make sure you’re paying attention to the learning objectives for each class or chapter. This is what the instructor will test you on in the exams.
  • Give yourself enough time! Creating a study schedule will keep you on track and make you feel less overwhelmed when you have 4 or 5 exams in a couple weeks.
    • This also means cramming is not good, information doesn’t stick for as long and also it can stress you out even more if you’re not understanding it as quickly as you’re expecting.
    • It may look like it is impossible to study that early but if you plan and schedule things accordingly, you can be closer to this goal than if you spend the night before cramming always.
    • Try out different methods to see what works best for you and how you learn.
    • This also gives you time to ask for help from the instructor, teaching assistants or resource centres during non-peak times.
How should I study for practical exams (ie. application-based exams)?
  • Practice questions will be your best bet here. In these classes, you are expected to apply what you learned. This requires a deeper level of thinking than just writing down the definition of something but instead, how can you apply it and integrate the concepts to new situations? Can you draw conclusions from the concepts taught in class in a hypothetical situation?
  • Double check your learning objectives to make sure you know what you are expected to know!
How should I study for content-heavy exams?
  • Focus on the learning objectives and creating a schedule of which content you will tackle on which day.
  • You want to test yourself in various ways in active recall (ask yourself questions!) and keep yourself actively learning, not passively (don’t just read the textbook).
  • Content-heavy exams can seem overwhelming but breaking it up across a schedule will make it easier for you to manage.
  • On another level, you may be expected to integrate this knowledge so while you’re studying one chapter, think about:
    • How is this connected to the previous chapter?
    • How do they compliment each other?
    • How are they similar or different?
    • How does the previous chapter build on top of this one?
    • What are the themes or patterns?
  • Content-heavy exams will have a portion of memorizing information but be prepared to use that information - check your learning objectives to confirm this.

University Basics

What is a syllbus and why is it important? How do I make the most of it?
  • The syllabus is very important! It gives you all of the information in the course including:
    • Course description
    • Course learning outcomes
    • How you will be assessed
    • Important dates
    • Textbook information
    • Readings you need to do for each class and when
    • (usually) a schedule of what topics you will discuss and when
    • Late policies
    • Academic integrity policies
    • Absence policies
    • Your instructor’s office hours and contact information
    • (if applicable) Teaching assistant office hours and/or contact information
  • Read through the syllabus carefully to see what is expected of you in the course. Input all of the important dates from all of your syllabi into a calendar that you check regularly to make sure you complete all of the tasks and don’t fall behind.
Are there bonus assignments?
  • There are rarely bonus assignments in university. If it is not outlined in the syllabus then there aren’t any bonus assignments or “make-up” assignments if you did poorly in something.
Can I do a re-test if I get a low score?
  • Very unlikely. Instead, you should take a look at the exam and see the gaps in your learning and work together with the instructor to see how you can improve for the next one along with what resources they recommend you to get help.
What can I do if I'm experience academic challenges (e.g. did poorly on an assessment, falling behind in class, etc)?
  • There are a variety of resources you can use but there are a few things you need to figure out first:
    • What is it that you are struggling with specifically? Is it one concept, a chapter, assignment, application-based questions, concept questions. Spend some time to figure this out.
    • See what resources are available for the course and field. Drop by instructor office hours and/or teaching assistants. Is there a learning centre available (e.g. Chem Resource Centre, Biology Resource Centre, Physics Help Centre, and Math Learning Centre are a few examples)?
  • This video may also be helpful if you are feeling stressed about your academic performance:
    • 7 min - How to Bounce Back from Failure
    • You can also watch a video we created about midterm failure??
Will one bad assignemnt or one bad exam impact my future?
    • No! It may seem like the end of the world and in that moment it can really feel like your world is falling apart! BUT... if it is one assignment, it is actually a lot smaller than you think. For anything that requires looking a transcript, they don’t see how you did on each assessment in the course, they only see the final grade. If you do poorly in one class, it is not the end of the world but think about how you can improve for the future. If there are patterns showing such as multiple failing grades each and every year, then that can indicate a bigger problem. What you want to show is that although you did poorly in a class or may even have failed a class, how can you bounce back and talk about this as a learning experience? This will also help you build resilience and take failure as learning moments.
  • That failure or low grade indicates that you need to do something different and figuring out what you need to do differently is the learning aspect! As you encounter these set-backs, you will also know how to cope better, learn proactive strategies that work for you and gain wisdom.
  • A lot of faculty members you see during your degree have had their fair share of academic challenges,you can see some of our faculty members talking about this here
How do I maintain academic integrity?
  • Maintaining integrity is more than just your coursework but also how you act within a workplace.

These tutors can provide feedback for undergraduate, independent written assignments for up to three drafts (take-home exams, group work and cover letter/resume (unless for an assignment) is not accepted). Learn more here. Log into the UBC tutor portal here.

If I have a question about one of my courses, should I e-mail the instructor? What's the best way to do it?
  • First, check the following:
    • Course syllabus
    • Canvas course announcements, discussion section
    • Lecture slides
    • Ask your peers in the class
    • Ask the Teaching Assistant for your section/course
  • If you still cannot find an answer, create a professional e-mail that includes the course and section (you can find your section number after the course name, for example CHEM 121 003, you are in section “003”) in the title of your email along with your question in a concise manner.
    • Please keep in mind that your instructor may take 2-3 days to respond so be patient.
    • For example (be mindful of including white space in your e-mail, it will make it a lot easier to read):
Dear Dr. Smith (for those with PhD's, otherwise "Instructor Smith" would suffice),   I am writing because I want to clarify the midterm exam in CHEM 121 (section 003). I wanted to ask if you are available during your office hours this week or at another time to review question #3 in the short answer section along with study strategies and how to do better next time.   Warm regards, Jane Doe
